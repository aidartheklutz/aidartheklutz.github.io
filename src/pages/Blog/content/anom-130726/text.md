For years, organized crime groups around the world relied on encrypted phones to stay hidden from law enforcement. These devices were designed with one purpose in mind: allowing criminals to communicate securely while making surveillance as difficult as possible. After the shutdown of several encrypted phone providers, many turned to a new platform called ANOM, believing it offered the same level of protection.

What its users didn't know was that ANOM had been secretly created and operated as part of an international undercover investigation. Every message sent through the platform was quietly copied to law enforcement, allowing investigators to monitor criminal activity for years without users becoming suspicious. By the time the operation ended on 8 June 2021, authorities had collected more than 27 million messages, leading to hundreds of arrests across 16 countries and making ANOM one of the most successful undercover operations ever carried out against organized crime.

## It All Began With Phantom Secure

In 2015, investigators in the United States were examining the organizer of a gambling ring. It turned out that the suspect was generating most of his illegal income through drug trafficking while deliberately keeping the gambling and trafficking operations separate. To protect his communications, he relied on an encrypted device designed to keep his conversations hidden.

Encrypted phones were considered one of the most powerful tools available to organized crime. Using custom mobile devices that ensured complete privacy, criminal groups around the world could coordinate drug trafficking, money laundering, and other illegal activities beyond the reach of law enforcement.

**Phantom Secure** was a major player in this market. This Canada-based company sold modified Blackberry phones with no GPS navigation, microphone, or camera. The internet and standard messaging services were also stripped away. Instead, the phones included an encrypted email service and could communicate only with other Phantom Secure devices, creating an isolated network.

Phantom Secure's ecosystem was completely closed to the general public: the company allowed customers to purchase a phone only if they were personally referred by an existing client. Their devices were purpose-built, marketed, and distributed exclusively for transnational criminal groups, offering secure encrypted communications to high-level drug traffickers and leaders of organized crime syndicates. According to Nicholas Cheviron, an FBI special agent, Phantom Secure's product had been used to plan all sorts of crimes, ranging from money laundering and drug trafficking to assault and murder.

In March 2018, the FBI arrested Vincent Ramos, the CEO and co-founder of Phantom Secure. However, Ramos himself was only part of the target. The Bureau was far more interested in the encrypted messages flowing through Phantom Secure's servers, believing they could expose entire criminal networks. Investigators offered Ramos a deal: avoid prison in exchange for installing a backdoor that would give the FBI access to the company's infrastructure.

## The Escape

The federal agents negotiated with Vincent Ramos for three days inside a hotel suite in Las Vegas. Finally, when he seemed ready to cooperate, the officials decided to transport Ramos to San Diego to continue the investigation there. Before leaving, however, they agreed to get some rest -- one agent watched Ramos while the others went to bed.

In the middle of the night, the agents discovered that Vincent Ramos had disappeared. It still remains unclear exactly how he escaped. The FBI launched a massive manhunt, and Ramos was found and arrested not long after. But while on the run, he managed to shut down a key server that the FBI would have needed for their backdoor, toppling the Bureau's plans of secretly gaining access to Phantom Secure's ecosystem.

## FBI's Tech Startup

Shortly after the incident, federal prosecutor Andrew P. Young received a weird phone call. The call came from a lawyer representing a man known by the pseudonym "Afgoo", a longtime encrypted phone developer involved with criminal organizations. Facing criminal charges himself, Afgoo had decided to cooperate with the FBI in exchange for a reduced sentence. Before approaching the Bureau, he had been developing a next-generation encrypted communication system for criminal networks. The lawyer's proposal was unlike anything Young had heard before: instead of trying to compromise another encrypted phone company from the outside, the FBI could simply run one themselves.

For Young, the offer arrived at exactly the right moment. The Phantom Secure operation had fallen apart after Ramos escaped and disabled the server the FBI needed. Running their own encrypted phone company suddenly looked like a second chance. The new company was named ANOM (elegantly stylized as ΛNØM), probably as a play on the word "anonymous". The FBI named the operation "Trojan Shield", while the Australian Federal Police called it "Ironside".

## A Quick Look at the ΛNØM phone

The ANOM devices were based on regular Google Pixel phones but came with a custom operating system called ArcaneOS. This specially modified ROM disabled normal functions such as voice telephony, email, or location services. It also included several privacy-focused features like PIN entry screen scrambling to randomize the layout of the numbers, the deletion of all information on the phone if a specific PIN was entered, and the option for automatic deletion of all data if the phone went unused for a specific period of time.

The phone had two modes: the main mode and the decoy mode. Users could set up two separate PIN codes, and the device would boot into different modes depending on which code was entered. In decoy mode, the interface mimicked an ordinary Android phone complete with many generic apps. These apps were mostly non-functional dummies, included purely to avoid raising suspicion.

This encrypted messaging app became the core feature of the device and was used extensively by criminal organizations. Each device also had a fixed identification number assigned to the user, which helped link messages from the same person.

## Distribution of ΛNØM

The first ANOM devices were offered by Afgoo to three former sellers of Phantom Secure in October 2018. About 50 devices were distributed in Australia for beta testing around that time. Every single one ended up being used for criminal activities, primarily by organised criminal gangs.

Distribution grew through word of mouth and was quietly encouraged by undercover agents. Drug trafficker Hakan Ayik was identified as a trusted figure who could successfully promote the platform on the black market. Without his knowledge, authorities encouraged him to use and sell the devices, significantly expanding their reach. After users requested smaller and newer phones, updated devices were designed and sold, with customer service and technical support also provided.

About 125 devices were shipped to different drop-off points in the United States in 2020. After a slow start, adoption accelerated from mid-2019. By May 2021, a total of 11800 devices had been distributed, with around 9000 actively in use across more than 100 countries. The most commonly used languages on the app were Dutch, German, and Swedish.

## How the Operation Went

The most important part of the operation was how the FBI secretly collected the messages. From the start, ANOM was designed to send a hidden copy of every message to special servers known as iBot servers. Because US laws limited how the FBI could handle data involving people in the United States, those servers were located overseas.

At first, the FBI relied on the Australian Federal Police to receive and process the data. In the summer of 2019, investigators arranged international legal agreements that allowed Lithuania to join the operation. By the end of 2019, copies of ANOM messages were being sent to servers in Lithuania, and from January 2020 onward, investigators received new batches of messages several times each week.

Conversations revealed drug shipments, money laundering schemes, weapons deals, and even discussions about murders and planned assassinations. In some cases, law enforcement had to act quickly to prevent violent attacks before they happened. However, investigators could not reveal ANOM as the source of their intelligence during these cases.

The FBI also put safeguards in place to comply with US law, particularly when messages involved people in the United States. Meanwhile, ANOM users continued to trust the platform, unaware that law enforcement was quietly collecting their communications. By the end of the operation, investigators had gathered more than **27 million messages** in 45 languages. An anonymous blog post published in March 2021 raised concerns about ANOM's security, but by that point the investigation was already nearing its conclusion.

## The Arrests and Operation Results

On 8 June 2021, the operation entered its final phase. Law enforcement agencies in 16 countries carried out hundreds of coordinated raids at nearly the same time. More than 800 people were arrested, over 700 locations were searched, and investigators spent months analyzing the evidence collected during the operation. It became one of the largest and most complex investigations ever conducted against organized crime groups using encrypted phones.

The raids led to major seizures around the world. Authorities confiscated nearly 40 tons of drugs, 250 firearms, 55 luxury vehicles, and more than $48 million in cash and cryptocurrencies. Australia accounted for 224 arrests and the seizure of assets worth more than 45 million Australian Dollars. New Zealand made 35 arrests and laid nearly 900 charges. Germany, the Netherlands, and Sweden also carried out dozens of arrests while dismantling drug production sites and trafficking networks. In the United States, prosecutors indicted 17 foreign nationals, including Hakan Ayik.

The FBI, Australian Federal Police, and Europol have released official announcements revealing that ANOM had been controlled by authorities. This surprised many users who believed they had been communicating through a secure criminal-only platform.

Perhaps the operation's greatest success was the intelligence it produced. Because investigators secretly controlled the platform, they were able to observe how major criminal organizations communicated and coordinated their activities. The operation also destroyed criminals' confidence in encrypted phone services.

## Conclusion

The ANOM operation showed how law enforcement was adapting to the growing use of encrypted communication by criminal groups. Rather than attempting to break into an existing network, investigators gained access by creating a platform that criminals themselves trusted. The operation provided authorities with a large amount of intelligence on international criminal activity and allowed them to raid hundreds of criminal organizations.
