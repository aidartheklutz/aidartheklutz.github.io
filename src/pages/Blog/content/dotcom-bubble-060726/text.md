In the second half of the 1990s, the internet began to develop rapidly. More and more people were buying personal computers, and companies were massively moving their businesses online. This went as far as to the point where the absence of a website for a company was already considered a sign of backwardness.

The internet was perceived as a technology that would completely change the economy. Investors expected to make quick money on internet projects and began pouring in funds at an enormous speed. New startups appeared every day. Valuations of internet companies grew faster than the companies themselves could develop. At the same time, few people understood how they were supposed to make stable profits. By 1999, about 39% of all venture capital was going into internet startups, and their main market became the [NASDAQ exchange](https://www.nasdaq.com/).

## The Bubble Inflates

The mechanics of growth looked strange, but for some time it worked. A company would attract investments, spend them on advertising and attracting an audience. The more users came and the more attention was gathered around the company, the higher the stock price became. And the higher the stock price, the easier it was to attract new money. This created a closed loop where growth fed itself.

Profits were often postponed for later: companies first aimed to capture the market and only then figure out how to make money. Some companies openly stated that revenue didn't matter right now. People bought stocks not because of the companies' current profits, but because of expectations of further growth. Of course, not everyone believed in endless growth: in 1996, the head of the US Federal Reserve, Alan Greenspan, called this ["irrational exuberance"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_exuberance).

**This is how a financial "bubble" emerges:** when asset prices rise not because of real business results, but because of expectations that they will continue to grow. Stocks become more expensive simply because people expect to sell them even more expensively tomorrow. This bubble was nicknamed the "dot-com bubble" because it was enough for companies to add ".com" to their name to attract investments.

A vivid symbol of the era appeared in January 2000: 14 internet companies spent huge sums on advertising during the [Super Bowl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Bowl), the main sporting event in the United States. Many of them had almost no profits but continued to spend millions because the market still rewarded the appearance of growth.

## Failed Startups

Let's look at examples of internet projects born from the dot-com bubble: Pets.com, TheGlobe.com, Webvan.com, Kozmo.com, and eToys.com. All of them went bankrupt in the early 2000s.

### Pets.com

<small>Pets.com beloved mascot -- a sock puppet. _[Image source](https://museumoffailure.com/exhibition/pets-com)_</small>
<!-- prettier-ignore-end -->

Pets.com actively grew during the dot-com boom, attracted major investors, and spent huge amounts on flashy advertising featuring a sock puppet mascot during the Super Bowl. However, most people were still not ready to buy pet products online. To attract customers, the company sharply lowered prices and introduced free shipping, which led to enormous losses when selling goods in bulk -- such a business model turned out to be unsustainable.

### TheGlobe.com

<!-- prettier-ignore-start -->
![TheGlobe.com home page](/blog-pictures/blog-dotcom-bubble-img1.webp)
<small>Screenshot of TheGlobe.com homepage. _[Image source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Theglobe.com,_screeshoot,_2012-05-20.png)_</small>
<!-- prettier-ignore-end -->

The company created one of the first social networks: users could create profiles, chat, and keep online diaries. In 1998, it went public, and on the first day the stocks increased by 606%. Founder Stephan Paternot became a media star and boasted in a CNN interview: "Got the girl. Got the money. Now I'm ready to live a disgusting, frivolous life". By 2001, the stocks had fallen almost to zero, the company had lost almost everything, and in 2001 it effectively ceased to exist. The money ran out, and a monetization model never appeared.

### Webvan.com

<!-- prettier-ignore-start -->
![Webvan delivery vans](/blog-pictures/blog-dotcom-bubble-img2.webp)
<small>Webvan delivery trucks next to their warehouse. _[Image source](https://streetfins.com/webvan-the-dotcom-bubbles-biggest-bust/)_</small>
<!-- prettier-ignore-end -->

An online supermarket that wanted to revolutionize grocery delivery. The company built huge automated warehouses and promised delivery within 30 minutes. In two years, it managed to attract about $1.2 billion in investments and go public. However, expenses were enormous, and demand was insufficient. In 2001, Webvan went bankrupt, closing with nearly a billion dollars in losses. Later, a similar model was successfully implemented by Amazon Fresh.

### Kozmo.com

<!-- prettier-ignore-start -->
![Kozmo.com delivery bag](/blog-pictures/blog-dotcom-bubble-img3.webp)
<small>Delivery bag of a Kozmo.com employee. _[Image source](https://affiliatetip.com/e-dreams-a-documentary-about-the-days-of-kozmo-com-is-on-netflix/)_</small>
<!-- prettier-ignore-end -->

A service for "internet delivery of anything within an hour" with no minimum order amount. They promised to deliver DVDs, food, ice cream, magazines, and other goods almost instantly. They attracted about $250 million, had huge warehouses, and an army of couriers on bicycles and cars. The idea seemed brilliant, but in reality, free delivery of small items was ruining the company. It closed in 2001.

### eToys.com

<!-- prettier-ignore-start -->
![eToys.com home page](/blog-pictures/blog-dotcom-bubble-img4.webp)
<small>Screenshot of the eToys.com homepage in the last days before closing in the UK. _[Image source](http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/business/1137699.stm)_</small>
<!-- prettier-ignore-end -->

This online toy store went public in 1999, and its market capitalization soared to $8 billion (with almost zero profit). The company spent huge amounts on marketing and warehouses, trying to compete with regular stores. By 2001, the stocks had collapsed, the company had accumulated huge debts, and it went bankrupt. Later the brand was purchased by "Toys 'R' Us", but the original eToys disappeared.

## Market Crash

However, any financial bubble eventually bursts. Several factors contributed to this at once:

- **The economy of Japan, the second-largest economy in the world at the time, entered a downturn.** Investors began to treat risky assets, including technology stocks, more cautiously.

- **The US Federal Reserve raised interest rates.** Loans became more expensive, and there was less money in the system.

- **It became increasingly obvious that most internet companies had no idea how to make money.** They might have had millions of users, but no clear way to turn that into stable revenue.

In March 2000, a massive sell-off of internet company stocks began. Panic spread, and the NASDAQ index started to fall -- by October it had lost more than 70% of its value. Thousands of companies closed because their businesses were almost entirely dependent on new investments. When the flow of money stopped, they simply ran out of funds to operate.

Major players were also affected. Amazon lost about 90% of its market value, Cisco -- more than 80%. The total losses for investors are estimated at about 5 trillion dollars.

## Conclusion

The internet didn't go anywhere, but after the bubble burst, it underwent a harsh purge: companies that lived only on expectations and venture money disappeared. Those with a real business model remained -- for example, eBay, Yahoo!, and Amazon. Google even managed to become the foundation of the modern internet.

The dot-com crash became a useful lesson. It showed that hype and user growth do not replace profit. After the bubble, investors became more cautious, and the surviving companies became stronger.
