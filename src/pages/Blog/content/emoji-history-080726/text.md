Some people call emoji the first truly global language. Unlike spoken languages, they do not belong to one country and do not require years of study. At first glance, emoji may seem like a simple invention, something that anybody could have come up with. However, their journey was far more complicated than it appears.

## The Beginning: Emoticons :-)

By the early 1980s, the Computer Science community at [Carnegie Mellon University](https://www.cmu.edu/) was already using an online bulletin board ("bboard") as a way for students, staff, and professors to communicate. It acted as an early version of modern internet forums: people shared announcements, asked questions, and discussed different topics ranging from everyday issues to politics and campus debates.

On the bboard, people often shared posts that were meant to be humorous, but text-based communication lacked body language and tone-of-voice cues. Because of this, readers could easily misunderstand a joke and interpret it as a serious statement instead. Some users suggested using special "joke markers" to explicitly indicate whether a post should not be taken seriously, and professor [Scott Fahlman](https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~sef/) joined the discussion with a simple but effective idea. He suggested using the character sequence `:-)` since it was easy to understand and could be handled by the ASCII-based computer terminals of the era. In the same post, he also proposed using `:-(` for posts that needed to be taken seriously.

Scott Fahlman's idea spread quickly, first around Carnegie Mellon and then to other universities. People started creating their own emoticons, including the "noseless" variants: `:)`, `:(`, `:D`, and many others. Thus, Scott Fahlman became known as the creator of the emoticon.

## Kaomoji (≧▽≦)

Emoticons were adapted and modified in Japan to become Kaomoji (顔文字), which translates to English as "face characters". They mixed ASCII with Japanese [half-width kana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-width_kana) characters, giving people much more room for creativity. While Western emoticons were meant to be read sideways, kaomoji were designed to be read horizontally, making them feel much more expressive. Instead of relying on a tilted smile, they often emphasized the eyes, which play an important role in conveying emotion in Japanese art and comics.

The creation of kaomoji is attributed to [Yasushi Wakabayashi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wakabayashi_Yasushi), the administrator of an online bulletin board related to people with disabilities. On June 20, 1986, he posted the oldest known instance of the `(^_^)` kaomoji. Just like with Scott Fahlman's emoticons, people online quickly began inventing countless variations of their own. ## Emoji 🤑

To provide an even simpler way to express emotions through text, in November 1997 the Japanese company [J-Phone (now called SoftBank)](https://www.softbank.jp/en/) released the **first ever** phone with an emoji keyboard: the SkyWalker DP-211SW. The phone's emoji set consisted of 90 distinct emoji characters.

The first **popular** emoji set was created in 1999 by [Shigetaka Kurita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shigetaka_Kurita) while he was working at the Japanese mobile telecommunications company NTT Docomo. At the time, Kurita was part of the team developing i-Mode, a mobile internet service that allowed users to browse websites, send emails, and make digital payments. To make communication easier on small phone screens, i-Mode introduced a set of 176 emoji, each measuring only 12x12 pixels. These tiny symbols were inspired by simple visual signs already common in Japanese culture, such as emotional expressions in manga and weather icons used in forecasts. Kurita's original set included various facial expressions along with everyday symbols he encountered in the city around him.

## The Need for Standardization

Kurita's success inspired competing Japanese mobile carriers to create emoji sets of their own. While the idea spread rapidly, there was one major problem: every company designed its own emoji and assigned them different internal codes. Sending a message from one carrier to another could result in the wrong emoji appearing, or no emoji at all. Developers were forced to manually match hundreds of symbols between incompatible systems, and even then many emoji had no direct equivalent.

The situation became even more complicated when smartphones with emoji support began arriving outside Japan. Apple added an emoji keyboard in the Japanese release of iOS 2.2 in 2008, but it became available worldwide only in 2011 with the launch of iOS 5.5. Google soon followed with Android.

The growing popularity of emoji also made it clear that they needed a universal standard. Unicode 6.0, released in October 2010, encoded hundreds of emoji into the Unicode Standard, allowing the same emoji to be represented consistently across different operating systems and devices. Companies were still free to draw emoji in their own artistic style, but the underlying code point became the same everywhere. A 😎 sent from an iPhone, for example, would still arrive as 😎 on an Android phone, even if the artwork looked different.

![sunglasses emoji across different platforms](/blog-pictures/blog-emoji-history-img6.webp) <small>How the sunglasses emoji appears on different platforms. _[Image source](https://www.facebook.com/Emojipedia/photos/its-nationalsunglassesday-which-pair-of-emoji-%EF%B8%8F-sunglasses-are-you-donning-today/2312627302217920/)_</small>

In 2014, the [Unicode Consortium](https://unicode.org/about.html) published formal guidelines for emoji design. They recommended that emoji preserve the same core appearance across platforms so users would recognize the same symbol regardless of whether they were designed by Apple, Google, Microsoft, Samsung, or another company.

![screenshot from a 2014 draft of a unicode technical report](/blog-pictures/blog-emoji-history-img5.webp) <small>A screenshot from a 2014 Unicode technical report. _[Image source](https://www.unicode.org/L2/L2014/14280-wd-uts51-draft.pdf)_</small>

## Conclusion

What started as a simple way to tell jokes apart from serious messages on a college bulletin board eventually evolved into emoji as we know them today. Emoticons allowed emotions to be expressed with simple keyboard characters, kaomoji expanded the idea into more expressive faces, and emoji replaced those text-based expressions with small pictures understood by millions of people around the world. Today, the Unicode Standard includes thousands of emoji, with new ones being added almost every year.
