I love history! Ever since middle school, when history classes had finally appeared in the curriculum, me and history have been been inseparable pals. Of course, I am definitely not like those "history nerds" you could find on internet forums or social media websites that argue about which WWII general contributed the most to the western front and make TikTok edits of Napoleon while having every single major date memorized in their little mind palace, but I still think I do have a great knowledge base of various historic events.

That said, recently our history teacher at the Technical School of Innovation, [N. Soulier](https://www.linkedin.com/in/061ab51b3/), assigned us a big research project, in which we had to analyze at least two primary sources and secondary sources. We were given around two weeks to complete it and in this post I am going to share how this exciting assignment went for me.

## Context

In the first semester (fall semester), we worked on the first half of this project. Basically, we had to pick any primary source (letter, newspaper, official document, diary, interview, etc.) and write a paper listing the information it gives us, regardless of its bias or inaccuracy -- our main goal was to just collect all the info provided in the primary source that describes the event or time period in any meaningful way. In the end of the project, we had to formulate a research question related to the event.

For the Primary Source Project, I picked the [letter sent by N. Khrushchev to J. F. Kennedy on October 26, 1962](https://microsites.jfklibrary.org/cmc/oct26/doc4.html) amid the Cuban Missile Crisis because of how interesing this period was to me. After all, humanity could have perished in a nuclear armageddon without their diplomatic efforts.

This primary source was pretty easy to work with: I just created a Google Document, copied the text of the letter into it, and highlighted every part of it I found useful. Then I compiled those finds into coherent paragraphs. It took me around three days to complete and I got the full score for my work! I made a lot of effort to make it both comprehensive and visually appealing. If you're curious, you can view the file of the Primary Source Project by [clicking the link here](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1z6lD3zhjoaRxG1THyHH57D5tYninJyoL/view?usp=drive_link).

In the spring semester, we moved on to the second part of the historical sources project. This time, it was even more interesting (and difficult).

## Working on the Secondary Source Project (SSP)

### Picking the sources

Besides the source we analyzed in the Primary Source Project, which was now considered our main source, we had to work with at least **one** additional primary source, and **two** secondary sources. The combination of these sources needed to be used to answer the research question created in the first project, or a new one.

In the end of the Primary Source Project, I came up with this research question: _«How do Khrushchev’s private letters to Kennedy differ from his public statements about the Cuban Missile Crisis, and what does this reveal about Soviet diplomatic strategy?»_. However, before starting the Secondary Source Project, I changed it to the following: _«How does the actual resolution to the Cuban Missile Crisis differ from the way it was presented to the American and international public and what does it reveal about the diplomatic strategy of the United States during the Cold War?»_. I kept the main idea of comparing public statements to secret messages between the leaders, but switched the subject of attention from the USSR to the USA. That was not due to any political reason, by the way, it was just easier work with American archives than the Soviet ones.

Then began the hunt for useful sources. I read other exchanges between Khrushchev and Kennedy, memorandums, messages from the white house, found articles about the secret missile swap between the global powers, interviews from experts, etc.

In the end, I gathered and decided to work with **FIVE** primary sources and three secondary sources, two of which were long articles and one was an interview with a Russian political science professor.

I quickly finished writing the literature review, where I briefly described how each source is relevant to the research project, and now it was time to actually begin working...

### Reading the sources

Of course, I knew how to conduct research and work with different sources. However, usually I didn't have to work with more than 3 sources at a time, and if I did then those usually were simple popular science articles (for example, for an astronomy class presentation). I needed to find a way to create clarity from chaos.

I began with... watching YouTube videos about how to do historical research. Does not sound very poetic, but I found [this really awesome video by Overly Sarcastic Productions](https://youtu.be/sigJwoeU6OI), which gave some great advice on how to work with historical sources. Inspired by it, I printed out all eight of my sources, took a highlighter and a notebook, and sat down to read and make notes. After finishing each document, I would put it in a folder.

Eventually, after two evenings of reading I was ready to move on to the next step. Yes, two evenings to read 5 documents and 3 articles might sound like too long, but I had a week to complete the project, as well as many other difficult assignments to work on.

### Writing the essay

And now it was time to finally use the collected information to write the project. My stack of highlighted papers and scribbled notes now had to be turned into an actual essay.

Writing felt like a completely different challenge, compared to finding and reading through sources. The reading and note-taking phase was relatively simple and straightforward: I could read the documents one by one and highlight whatever I found interesting and useful. Now, however, I had to figure out a way to connect all of those pieces together.

The process was quite overwhelming. I had so many details in mind that I wanted to include! For several hours, I rearranged quotations, moved paragraphs around, and rewrote my outline. I returned to my notes, went through them, and decided to make the secret missile swap between the United States and the Soviet Union the main argument of my research, because this was the most important thing hidden from the public. Finally, after gathering all of my thoughts, words just began typing themselves and writing the project turned into a suprisingly pleasurable activity.

### End result

After three days of writing, I finally finished the project and uploaded it to the school platform. It turned out unreasonably huge: the final result had **5160 words**, which exceeded the minimal word count of ~350 words by about **1374.3%!!!** Have I overdone it? Yes, but the project was so interesting to work on that I just didn't care. Ms. Soulier jokingly called it a "mini-college thesis" when I was defending my project, which I found really funny. I successfully earned the full score.

## Conclusion

I loved working on this project because it taught me a lot about analyzing primary sources and comparing them to other sources, both primary and secondary. Thanks to my history teacher for creating such a good way to engage students in learning historical research.

It allowed me to learn even more about the Cold War than I had known before, and encouraged me to read various articles. I have significantly improved my workflow and those research skills helped me work on different assignments as well (and will definitely help me in the future).

Besides practical skills, I also realized one important thing: if you're curious about something, do not hesitate to do an extra effort to express it. After all, curiosity is what drives innovation, and working on this project definitely didn't feel like a burden to me. I was curious and I was ready to work hard. Of course, it got a little boring closer to the middle, but I still liked the end result.
