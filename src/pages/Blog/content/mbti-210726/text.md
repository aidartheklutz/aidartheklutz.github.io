According to astrology, I'm a Scorpio. According to [16Personalities](https://www.16personalities.com/), I'm an INTP-T. According to a [My Little Pony quiz](https://www.idrlabs.com/my-little-pony/test.php), I'm 75% Twilight Sparkle and 90% Fluttershy. None of these descriptions should influence how people judge my abilities or potential, and yet some companies still use MBTI to assess candidates and make recruitment decisions.

## What is MBTI

In the late 19th century, Katharine Briggs was looking for ways she could apply laboratory science techniques she learned at the [Michigan Agricultural College](https://msu.edu/) to the process of raising her children. Katharine became fascinated by the personality differences she noticed among her family members and began developing her own ideas about human behavior and personality types. She wanted to create a practical way to better understand these differences and improve mutual understanding between people.

In 1921, the Swiss psychoanalyst Carl Gustav Jung published his book ["Psychological Types"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological_Types). His personality type theory looked at whether someone is more introverted or extraverted and examined how strongly each person relies on four core ways of experiencing life: Thinking (using logic and reason), Feeling (responding through emotions and values), Sensation (relying on the five senses), and Intuition (trusting gut feelings). Jung believed that the different combinations and strengths of these traits formed a useful "map" of personality, which made it easier for psychologists to understand patients and choose the best ways to work with them.

Katharine Briggs, who had been developing her own theory of personality types, encountered Jung's work in 1923 and used it to refine her ideas. In the 1940s, Katharine and her daughter Isabel Briggs Myers designed the Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI) along with a 172-item questionnaire. This indicator categorized people using the following preference pairs:

- Extraversion (E) or Introversion (I)
- Sensing (S) or Intuition (N)
- Thinking (T) or Feeling (F)
- Judging (J) or Perceiving (P)

Different combinations of these preference pairs (e.g., ESTJ, ISTP, INFP) form 16 distinct categories. Each category has its own label and description.

By the 1970s, MBTI was already being actively used -- for example, by students and staff at the Michigan Agricultural College. Its popularity skyrocketed with the growth of the internet, especially thanks to the British company Neris Analytics Limited, which launched [16personalities.com](https://www.16personalities.com/) in 2011. This website provides a 60-question online quiz with a simplified and more engaging version of MBTI. Nowadays, MBTI is one of the world's most widely recognized personality assessments.

## MBTI and People

Over time, MBTI has moved far beyond its original purpose and started being treated a lot like modern horoscopes. People share their four-letter types on social media, use them in dating profiles, and even make memes about how "INTPs can't handle emotions" or "ENFPs are chaotic sunshine". People find such memes fun and relatable. But this view of MBTI also turns a psychological tool into a silly identity badge.

Just like zodiac signs, MBTI offers quick, memorable descriptions that feel personally accurate. This has helped it spread rapidly online, especially through platforms like 16Personalities, which made the test accessible and shareable. MBTI has become a popular way for people to explain (and sometimes excuse) their behavior: "Sorry I'm late, I'm an INFP" or "Of course I overthink everything -- classic INTJ" (wow, very quirky). Sure, this popularization has made personality discussions more common and less intimidating, but it also risks oversimplifying human behavior.

## MBTI in Corporate Settings

Starting from the 1990s, more and more companies began using MBTI to search for "perfect candidates". Fortune 1000 is a list of 1000 biggest companies in the United States curated by the Fortune magazine. According to author of the book "The Personality Brokers" [Merve Emre](https://www.marketplace.org/story/2018/10/30/myers-briggs-system-evaluate-employees), roughly one in five companies in the Fortune 1000 list uses MBTI during the hiring process. Big consulting firms like McKinsey, Deloitte, and Accenture have also reportedly used it to evaluate whether candidates would be suitable for serving clients.

According to [JobCannon](https://jobcannon.io/blog/does-mbti-predict-job-performance), some companies go even further and screen candidates for specific personality types, saying things like "we only hire ENTJs for management" or giving MBTI results significant weight when making hiring decisions.

## Why MBTI Shouldn't Be Taken Too Seriously

### Your personality is complex

Carl Jung was a brilliant psychologist and psychiatrist who pioneered analytical psychology. However, his work on psychological types -- which inspired Katharine Briggs -- was largely theoretical and based on his clinical observations rather than the rigorous empirical research methods used in modern psychology. Jung saw his personality types as a helpful framework for understanding how different people perceive the world and process information. He never intended them to be used as strict, permanent labels that box people in. In fact, he warned against over-simplifying human nature.

> "There is no such thing as a pure extrovert or a pure introvert. Such a man would be in the lunatic asylum"

> -- Carl Jung

The MBTI, however, often moves away from this nuance. Instead of viewing personality traits as flexible tendencies that exist on a spectrum, it sorts people into fixed categories.

Imagine a test shows you are 51% introverted and 49% extraverted. That doesn't mean you are fundamentally one or the other. You might prefer solitude in some situations and enjoy being sociable in others (just like most people do, lol). But when the MBTI turns these small differences into a single clear-cut label, it can push people to think of themselves as belonging strictly to one category instead of embracing the natural complexity of their personality.

### The problem with personality labels

Personality tests like the Myers-Briggs give people clear labels that many take very seriously. People often start using them as a big part of their identity, saying things like "I am an INTJ" or "I am an ENFP".

The problem is that these labels can create tunnel vision. Once you accept one, you might stop noticing all the times it doesn't actually fit you. When you strongly identify with a label, you start believing it's always true, even though it isn't. In different situations you will often behave in ways that don't match the label at all. But because you see the label as "who you are", you tend to ignore those moments.

Labels can be useful for therapists in certain contexts. However, it's usually better not to give them to clients as fixed identities. Once the label becomes a major part of how someone sees themselves, it can make it much harder for them to change and grow. People also tend to make choices that reinforce the label instead of challenging it: "I'm an INTP so I have to be pragmatic all the time, even though this situation clearly requires me to be creative".

### MBTI has poor reliability

Psychologists evaluate personality tests using several important measures. One key measure is called test-retest reliability. This simply means that if you take the test today and then take it again a few weeks later, you should get roughly similar results. Of course, a bit of change is normal -- people aren't robots and our moods or situations can shift slightly. But your core personality doesn't flip completely from one week to the next, so a good test shouldn't give you wildly different outcomes either.

The MBTI struggles with this quite badly. A [study](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232494957_Cautionary_comments_regarding_the_Myers-Briggs_Type_Indicator) has shown that when people retake the test after about five weeks, around 50% of them end up with a different personality type than before. This level of inconsistency is considered unacceptable in serious psychological testing.

This problem happens because the MBTI forces people into strict categories even though many personality traits exist on a spectrum. If your score is right on the border between two types (again, let's say 51% introverted and 49% extraverted), even a small shift in how you feel that day can push you into a completely different category. Tiny changes suddenly create big differences in your "official" type.

### The publishers themselves don't recommend MBTI for hiring

Even the official Myers & Briggs Foundation is very clear about this: "It is not ethical to use the MBTI instrument for hiring or for deciding job assignments". The main reason is that the test simply isn't good at predicting how well someone will actually perform in a job.

This makes a lot of sense when you think about it. Personality is only one small piece of what makes someone successful at work. Skills, experience, motivation, and specific job requirements matter far more. Yet some companies still treat MBTI results as if they can reliably tell them who will be a great fit.

### MBTI ignores neuroticism (and other important traits)

One of the biggest weaknesses of the MBTI is what it chooses **not** to measure. A very important personality trait called [neuroticism](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/neuroticism) describes how much someone's mental life is filled with negative emotions like anxiety, sadness, or feelings of vulnerability. People who score higher on neuroticism tend to experience these emotions more often and more intensely. This trait is linked to many real-life outcomes: it can influence romantic relationships, career success, physical health, and even the risk of certain mental health challenges.

Modern personality models like the [Big Five](https://www.verywellmind.com/the-big-five-personality-dimensions-2795422) (also known as OCEAN) include neuroticism. The MBTI, however, completely leaves it out. None of the 16 MBTI types include anything too negative. This omission makes the test more commercially attractive (no one gets "bad news" about their results), but it also means MBTI gives an incomplete picture of personality. If the goal is to truly understand people, ignoring such a significant factor is a serious oversight.

## Why People Trust MBTI

Despite all these flaws, many people still say that their own MBTI results feel pretty accurate. If you feel this way too, you're definitely not alone. Here's why that happens.

First, people have a natural tendency to read themselves into vague descriptions. This is the same reason many believe their horoscope fits them perfectly -- even when they're accidentally reading the wrong sign. The MBTI takes advantage of this through something called [Barnum statements](https://www.britannica.com/science/Barnum-Effect). These are strategically vague descriptions that sound specific but are broad enough for almost anyone to relate to. The name comes from showman P. T. Barnum, who famously knew how to give people exactly what they wanted to hear.

On top of that, if you already want to believe the results or simply find them entertaining, [confirmation bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confirmation_bias) joins in. You notice the parts that match you and overlook the ones that don't. Combine this with the test's deliberate avoidance of anything negative (like neuroticism), and you get descriptions that feel insightful and positive without ever delivering uncomfortable truths.

In short, the MBTI is cleverly designed to feel personal and affirming. That's why it resonates with people so strongly: it tells us what we enjoy hearing about ourselves.

## Why Companies Still Use MBTI

MBTI's popularity in the corporate world persists for several understandable reasons: it is memorable and makes intuitive sense, it is non-threatening (there are no "good" or "bad" types), it can spark useful conversations about communication and teamwork, and it is easy and relatively cheap to administer. These are all legitimate reasons to use it for team-building exercises or general self-awareness.

However, using MBTI for actual hiring decisions is not evidence-based. MBTI does not reliably predict job performance, so it isn't actually selecting for competence. If a company wants a personality tool for hiring, the [Big Five](https://www.verywellmind.com/the-big-five-personality-dimensions-2795422) (also known as OCEAN) is a much stronger, scientifically supported choice.

## Conclusion

MBTI can be a nice conversation starter or a fun fact to add to your social media bio. It might even help you understand your friends a bit better or benefit team-building in workplaces. However, when it comes to hiring decisions or trying to define whom you really are, MBTI is simply not the right tool for the job. Your personality is way more complicated, changeable, and interesting than a four-letter code. Don't let it bind you inside a frame or become a big part of your identity.
